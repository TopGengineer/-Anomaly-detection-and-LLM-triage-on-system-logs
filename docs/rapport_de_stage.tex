\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
This report documents an internship carried out within OCP Group's
\emph{Digital Infrastructure and Operations}: the design, implementation, and
rigorous validation of an end-to-end anomaly-detection system for
machine-generated system logs, augmented with a stochastic-process model of
event timing and a large-language-model (LLM) triage layer. The system is
developed and evaluated entirely on public benchmark datasets (\code{loghub}
HDFS and BGL), making every result reproducible while remaining directly
relevant to the security-operations context of a large industrial group. The
pipeline comprises: automatic log parsing into structured events (Drain); an
unsupervised autoencoder detector benchmarked against an Isolation Forest;
a univariate Hawkes point process fitted by maximum likelihood to model the
self-excitation of log events, validated with the time-rescaling theorem and
evaluated as a source of detection features; an adaptive alerting threshold
based on extreme-value theory, with a distribution-free alternative,
stabilising the false-alarm rate under drift; and an LLM triage agent that
deduplicates alerts, correlates them into incidents, and produces ranked,
structured, explained incident reports. Validation follows a deliberately
strict protocol: a never-shuffled temporal train/test split, precision--recall
analysis rather than accuracy, false alarms per day at a fixed detection rate
as the headline metric, and explicit measurement of drift. Key results
include a detection PR-AUC of $0.987$ on HDFS ($\sim$20$\times$ fewer false
alarms than the baseline), a near-critical Hawkes branching ratio
($\eta\approx0.97$--$1.0$) on BGL, a measured halving of false alarms
attributable to timing features, and a demonstrated stabilisation of the
false-alarm rate under drift. Negative and nuanced findings are reported with
the same care as positive ones, as an explicit methodological choice.

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} anomaly detection, system logs, Hawkes process,
point processes, extreme-value theory, unsupervised learning, autoencoder,
LLM, security operations, OCP Group.

\chapter{General introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

\section{Context}
Modern industrial organisations run on software. Behind every mine
conveyor, every chemical unit, every logistics chain and every commercial
transaction of a group such as OCP stands a digital estate of servers,
networks, applications, and industrial control systems---and every element of
that estate continuously writes \emph{logs}: timestamped text records of what
it is doing. Logs are simultaneously the primary forensic record of an
infrastructure and an overwhelming torrent of data: a large organisation
produces millions of lines per day, in which the events that actually matter
---failures, misconfigurations, intrusions, cascading faults---are rare,
unlabelled, and buried.

This internship, hosted by OCP Group's \emph{Digital Infrastructure and
Operations}, addresses that problem end to end. It designs, implements,
validates, and documents a complete prototype that ingests raw logs and
produces a short, ranked, explained list of incidents. Beyond the
engineering, the internship had an explicit academic ambition, agreed with
both supervisors: to treat the subject as a case study in \emph{rigorous
time-series methodology}---unsupervised learning under extreme class
imbalance, point-process modelling of event timing, extreme-value methods
for alerting, and a validation discipline strict enough to be defensible in
front of a critical audience.

\section{Problem in one paragraph}
Given a stream of raw log lines, with no labelled examples of ``bad''
behaviour, the system must: structure the stream into events; learn what
normal activity looks like; score departures from normality; convert scores
into alerts whose false-alarm rate an operations team can live with, even as
the data drifts; and compress the surviving alerts into a handful of
prioritised, explained incidents. Each stage of that sentence maps to a
chapter of this report.

\section{Objectives of the internship}
The objectives fixed at the outset were:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item build a complete, reproducible pipeline: raw logs $\to$ structured
  events $\to$ unsupervised detection $\to$ calibrated alerting $\to$ triage
  $\to$ demonstrator;
  \item place a \emph{stochastic-process model} at the mathematical core: fit
  a Hawkes process to event timestamps, estimate its branching ratio,
  validate the fit with the time-rescaling theorem, and evaluate the
  resulting timing features for detection;
  \item apply a strict validation protocol throughout: temporal train/test
  split, precision--recall analysis, false alarms per day as headline metric,
  drift measurement;
  \item prototype an LLM-based triage agent producing structured, ranked,
  explained incidents;
  \item (stretch) investigate extreme-value theory for self-calibrating alert
  thresholds.
\end{enumerate}
All five objectives, including the stretch goal, were completed.

\section{Reading guide}
The report is organised in three parts.
\textbf{Part~I} (Chapters~\ref{ch:company}--\ref{ch:department}) presents the
host organisation---OCP Group, then the Digital Infrastructure and Operations

\section{Why log analytics matters to this entity}
An infrastructure-and-operations organisation lives in its telemetry.
Every layer it operates---compute, network, storage, platforms, security
appliances---emits logs continuously, and three structural difficulties
define the analytical problem, none specific to OCP but all amplified by
industrial scale:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item \textbf{Volume asymmetry.} Events worth human attention are a
  vanishing fraction ($\sim10^{-3}$ or less) of millions of daily lines;
  manual review is impossible and naive alerting drowns analysts in false
  positives---the well-documented ``alert fatigue'' of security operations.
  \item \textbf{Absence of labels.} New failure modes and attack techniques
  appear continuously; no labelled catalogue can be exhaustive. Methods must
  learn \emph{normality} and flag departures---the unsupervised paradigm.
  \item \textbf{Temporal clustering.} Real incidents are \emph{cascades}: one
  root cause triggers bursts of correlated events across systems. Timing
  carries information, yet most deployed tooling treats events as
  independent points.
\end{enumerate}
These three difficulties are, term for term, the three axes of this project:
unsupervised detection (\S\ref{sec:impl-detection}), explicit modelling of
temporal clustering (\S\ref{sec:impl-hawkes}), and volume reduction by
correlation and triage (\S\ref{sec:impl-triage}).

\section{Anatomy of a security-operations workflow}
To situate the project precisely, consider the canonical workflow of a
security-operations capability, common to all large organisations:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item \textbf{Collection}: logs and telemetry are centralised from
  servers, network equipment, applications, and security appliances;
  \item \textbf{Normalisation}: heterogeneous formats are parsed into
  structured events;
  \item \textbf{Detection}: rules and models score events or aggregates
  for suspicion;
  \item \textbf{Alerting}: scores exceeding thresholds become alerts;
  \item \textbf{Triage}: analysts deduplicate, group, prioritise, and
  investigate alerts;
  \item \textbf{Response and feedback}: incidents are remediated and
  lessons fed back into detection content.
\end{enumerate}
The pain points concentrate in stages 3--5: rule-based detection misses
novel behaviour (hence unsupervised models); static thresholds break under
drift (hence adaptive alerting); and per-alert handling drowns analysts
(hence automated correlation and triage). The project's components are a
one-to-one mapping onto these three pain points---stages 2 through 5 of
this workflow are precisely what the prototype implements end to end on
public data.

\chapter{Problem statement and state of the art}
\label{ch:stateofart}

\section{Formal problem statement}
\label{sec:problem}
Let a log source emit raw text lines. After parsing, the stream becomes a
marked point pattern $\{(t_i,e_i)\}_{i\ge1}$: $t_i\in\mathbb{R}_+$ is a
timestamp and $e_i\in\{1,\dots,K\}$ an \emph{event type} from a finite
vocabulary of $K$ templates. Events are grouped into \emph{units of
analysis} $u$ (sessions or fixed windows), each with a latent state
$y_u\in\{0,1\}$ (normal / anomalous). The task decomposes into four
sub-problems:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item \textbf{Scoring} (unsupervised): learn $s(u)\in\mathbb{R}$ from
  \emph{unlabelled} data such that anomalous units rank systematically
  higher; labels serve only for evaluation.
  \item \textbf{Timing modelling}: model the law of the timestamps
  $\{t_i\}$ themselves, both as a scientific object (how do events beget
  events?) and as a feature source for scoring.
  \item \textbf{Alerting}: map scores to binary alerts through a threshold
  whose false-alarm behaviour is controlled \emph{through time}, i.e.\
  robust to drift of the score distribution.
  \item \textbf{Triage}: compress the alert stream into few, ranked,
  explained incidents.
\end{enumerate}
Three empirical properties constrain all four: extreme class imbalance
($\Pr[y_u{=}1]\sim10^{-3}$--$10^{-1}$); non-stationarity at the scale of
hours; and strong temporal clustering of events. The validation protocol
(\S\ref{sec:validation-protocol}) is built around exactly these properties.

\section{State of the art}
\label{sec:sota}

\subsection{Log parsing}
Parsing free text into a finite template vocabulary is a mature problem
with public benchmarks. \keyword{Drain}~\cite{drain} maintains a
fixed-depth parse tree whose internal nodes route lines by length and
leading tokens and whose leaves hold template clusters; each line is
assigned online, in near-constant time, to the most similar cluster or to a
new one. Drain is the accuracy/speed reference of the \code{logparser}
toolkit, evaluated across sixteen datasets in \code{loghub}~\cite{loghub},
and ships with benchmark configurations per dataset---which this project
adopts unchanged for comparability.

\subsection{Log anomaly detection}
Two families dominate. \emph{Count-based} methods aggregate each
session/window into an event-count vector and apply an unsupervised
detector: PCA reconstruction, invariant mining, clustering, or Isolation
Forest~\cite{iforest}; the \code{loglizer} study~\cite{loglizer}
systematises them. \emph{Sequence-based} deep methods model order:
DeepLog~\cite{deeplog} trains an LSTM to predict the next template and
flags low-probability continuations; LogAnomaly~\cite{loganomaly} adds
template semantics; autoencoders on count vectors are the standard
unsupervised neural baseline. On the HDFS benchmark these methods
routinely reach F1/PR-AUC of $0.95$--$0.99$; the result is among the most
reproduced in the field---an important fact for positioning
(\S\ref{sec:positioning}).

\subsection{Point processes for event streams}
The Hawkes process~\cite{hawkes} is the canonical self-exciting point
process, with mature theory: likelihood-based estimation, the
cluster/branching representation, exact simulation by thinning~\cite{ogata},
and goodness of fit via the time-rescaling theorem~\cite{timerescaling};
textbook treatments include Daley and Vere-Jones~\cite{daley}. It is
standard in seismology (aftershock modelling), financial microstructure
(order-flow), and social-media diffusion. Its use as a \emph{feature
source for log anomaly detection} is uncommon: the dominant log-analysis
paradigm discards fine-grained timing altogether, which is precisely the
gap this project probes.

\subsection{Thresholding and extreme values}
Converting scores to alerts is usually treated as an afterthought (fixed
quantiles), yet it is where deployments fail under drift. Extreme-value
theory provides the principled tool: the Pickands--Balkema--de~Haan
theorem~\cite{pickands} (textbook: Coles~\cite{coles}) characterises
threshold exceedances by the Generalized Pareto law, and the
SPOT/DSPOT algorithms~\cite{spot} apply it to streaming anomaly scores
with re-estimation over time.

\subsection{LLM-assisted triage}
Employing large language models to summarise, categorise, and prioritise
security alerts is an emerging, practice-driven area. Two design lessons
recur in early literature and industrial reports: constrain the model to a
\emph{structured output schema} (free text is not actionable downstream),
and apply LLMs \emph{after} deterministic reduction---deduplication and
correlation---so that cost scales with incidents, not raw alerts. Both
lessons are adopted here.

\chapter{Theoretical background}
\label{ch:theory}

This chapter develops, from first principles, every mathematical tool used
in Part~III. A reader comfortable with probability at engineering-school
level should be able to follow all of Part~III from this chapter alone.

\section{Evaluation under extreme class imbalance}
\label{sec:theory-metrics}

\subsection{Why accuracy fails}
With $P$ positives and $N$ negatives, $P\ll N$, the degenerate classifier
``always normal'' attains accuracy $N/(N{+}P)$---above $97\%$ on our data
---while detecting nothing. Any metric dominated by true negatives is
uninformative here; evaluation must focus on the rare class.

\subsection{Precision, recall, and the PR curve}
For a threshold $\theta$ on the score, with TP, FP, FN the usual counts:
\[
\mathrm{precision}(\theta)=\frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP}},
\qquad
\mathrm{recall}(\theta)=\frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FN}}.
\]
Sweeping $\theta$ traces the PR curve; its area (\keyword{PR-AUC},
equivalently average precision) summarises ranking quality. Two facts make
it the right choice under imbalance. First, its \emph{chance level equals
the prevalence}: a scorer independent of the labels has, at any recall,
expected precision $P/(P{+}N)$, so the whole random curve is the horizontal
line at the positive rate, and any lift above it is interpretable. Second,
it ignores true negatives entirely, so the huge $N$ cannot flatter it.
ROC-AUC, in contrast, normalises the false-positive count by $N$; with
$N\sim10^5$, tens of thousands of false alarms move the ROC curve
imperceptibly while destroying practical utility---which is why ROC figures
are not reported in this work.

\subsection{The operational metric}
Operations teams commit to a detection level and live with the alert
volume. The headline metric is therefore \keyword{false alarms per day at
fixed recall}: fix recall at $0.90$ (the smallest threshold whose top-scored
set captures $90\%$ of positives), count false positives above it, and
divide by the \emph{measured} duration of the test period. Reporting the
duration honestly matters: test units are rarely uniform in time, and
assuming a nominal period silently rescales the metric.

\section{Temporal validation and leakage}
\label{sec:theory-temporal}
The purpose of a test set is to estimate performance on \emph{future} data.
Random shuffling interleaves training and test in time, allowing
information from the test period (shared regimes, shared incidents,
long-lived sessions) to reach the model---\keyword{data leakage}---and
inflating every metric. The protocol here is a strict \keyword{temporal
split}: order units by start time, train on the earliest fraction, test on
the strictly later remainder, and never shuffle. The prohibition covers
\emph{everything} fitted on data: normalisation statistics, feature
vocabularies, and alert thresholds are all computed on the training period
only. The cost is honesty: temporal evaluation yields lower, real numbers,
and exposes drift instead of averaging it away.

\section{Log parsing: the Drain algorithm}
\label{sec:theory-drain}
Because every later stage consumes Drain's output, its mechanism deserves a
precise description. Drain~\cite{drain} maintains a parse tree of fixed
depth. An incoming line is first preprocessed by \emph{masking}: known
variable patterns (block identifiers, IP addresses, numbers) are replaced by
a wildcard, using dataset-specific regular expressions. The masked line is
then routed: the first tree level branches on the token \emph{count} of the
line; subsequent levels branch on the first few tokens; the leaf reached
holds a list of \emph{template clusters}. The line is compared to each
cluster's template by token-wise similarity
$\mathrm{sim}=\frac{1}{L}\sum_{k=1}^{L}\mathbf{1}\{w_k=\tilde w_k\}$
(wildcards match anything); if the best similarity exceeds a threshold
(0.5 in the benchmark configuration) the line joins that cluster and
mismatching tokens become wildcards, otherwise a new cluster is created.
\emph{Worked example}: the masked lines
``\code{Received block <*> of size <*> from <*>}'' and
``\code{Received block <*> of size <*> from <*>}'' route to the same leaf
(same length, same head tokens) and merge at similarity 1; whereas
``\code{Deleting block <*> file <*>}'' differs in length and lands in a
different branch, founding its own template. The result is an online,
near-constant-time parser whose output vocabulary is stable and
human-readable---properties that machine-learned parsers do not always
offer, and the reason Drain remains the reference.

\section{Unsupervised detectors}
\label{sec:theory-detectors}

\subsection{Why the chance level of PR-AUC equals the prevalence}
A short derivation used repeatedly when reading results. If scores are
independent of labels, then for any threshold the flagged set is a uniformly
random subset of the units; the expected fraction of true positives inside
it equals the population prevalence $\pi=P/(P{+}N)$. Hence expected
precision is $\pi$ \emph{at every recall}, the random PR curve is the
horizontal line at height $\pi$, and its area is $\pi$. This is why we
always quote PR-AUC \emph{together with} the prevalence (e.g.\ ``0.987
against a chance level of 0.022'')---the ratio, not the absolute value,
carries the meaning.

\subsection{Isolation Forest}
Isolation Forest~\cite{iforest} scores by \emph{ease of isolation}. An
isolation tree partitions the data by recursive random splits (random
feature, random cut point); a point's \emph{path length} is the number of
splits until it sits alone. Points in dense regions require many splits;
outliers few. Scores aggregate path lengths over an ensemble of trees built
on subsamples, normalised by the expected path length of an unsuccessful
binary-search-tree lookup, $c(n)=2H_{n-1}-2(n{-}1)/n$ with
$H_m$ the harmonic number, so that scores are comparable across sample
sizes. Strengths: no labels, near-linear cost, no distributional
assumptions---the natural baseline. Limits: axis-aligned splits capture
inter-feature structure only coarsely, which is exactly where a neural
detector can improve.

\subsection{Autoencoders}
An \keyword{autoencoder} composes an encoder
$f_\theta:\mathbb{R}^d\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^k$ and decoder
$g_\phi:\mathbb{R}^k\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^d$, $k\ll d$, trained to minimise
reconstruction error $\lVert x-g_\phi(f_\theta(x))\rVert^2$. The bottleneck
forbids the identity; minimising the loss forces the network to encode the
dominant regularities of the training distribution. Trained
\emph{exclusively on normal units}, it reconstructs normal patterns
accurately and abnormal patterns poorly; the per-unit MSE is the anomaly
score. Practical safeguards used in this work: inputs are
$\log(1{+}x)$-transformed (heavy-tailed counts) and standardised with
statistics from \emph{normal training data only}; training uses Adam with
early stopping on a held-out slice of the normal training set (never on
anything from the test period); and the score orientation (higher = more
anomalous) matches the baseline so a single evaluation harness serves both.

\section{Point processes and the Hawkes model}
\label{sec:theory-hawkes}

\subsection{Counting processes and conditional intensity}
A temporal point process is a random sequence of times
$0<t_1<t_2<\cdots$, equivalently a counting process
$N(t)=\#\{i:t_i\le t\}$. Its dynamics are specified by the
\keyword{conditional intensity}
\[
\lambda(t)=\lim_{\Delta\downarrow0}
\frac{\Pr[N(t{+}\Delta)-N(t)=1\mid\mathcal{H}_t]}{\Delta},
\]
the instantaneous event rate given the history $\mathcal{H}_t$. The
homogeneous Poisson process is the memoryless case
$\lambda\equiv\mu$: exponential i.i.d.\ gaps, independent counts, Fano
factor (variance/mean of interval counts) equal to $1$. Measured Fano
factors of $5$--$2000$ on our data (\S\ref{sec:impl-eda}) rule it out and
call for history dependence.

\subsection{The Hawkes process and its branching structure}
The exponential-kernel \keyword{Hawkes process}~\cite{hawkes} sets
\begin{equation}
\lambda(t)=\mu+\sum_{t_i<t}\alpha e^{-\beta(t-t_i)},\qquad
\mu,\alpha,\beta>0 .
\label{eq:hawkes}
\end{equation}
Each event lifts the intensity by $\alpha$; the lift decays with timescale
$1/\beta$. The process has an exact \emph{cluster representation}:
immigrants arrive as a Poisson process of rate $\mu$, and every event
(immigrant or descendant) independently produces offspring as an
inhomogeneous Poisson process with rate $\alpha e^{-\beta s}$ after it. The
expected number of direct offspring per event, the \keyword{branching
ratio}, is
\begin{equation}
\eta=\int_0^\infty\alpha e^{-\beta s}\,ds=\frac{\alpha}{\beta}.
\end{equation}
Galton--Watson theory then gives the phase diagram: for $\eta<1$
(subcritical) cascades are a.s.\ finite with mean total size
$1/(1-\eta)$ per immigrant, and the process is stationary with mean rate
\[
\bar\lambda=\frac{\mu}{1-\eta}
\quad\text{(immigrants $\mu$, each spawning a cascade of mean size
$\tfrac{1}{1-\eta}$),}
\]
whereas $\eta\ge1$ is (super)critical: cascades become self-sustaining and
the event count explodes. Thus $\eta$ condenses ``how cascade-prone is this
stream?'' into a single interpretable number, and its proximity to $1$
measures how close the system is to self-sustaining avalanches.

\subsection{Likelihood, recursion, and estimation}
For events $\{t_i\}_{i=1}^n$ on $[0,T]$ the log-likelihood of any intensity
model is
\begin{equation}
\ell=\sum_{i=1}^n\log\lambda(t_i)-\int_0^T\lambda(s)\,ds ,
\label{eq:ll}
\end{equation}
the first term rewarding intensity placed on actual events, the second
(the expected event count) penalising indiscriminate intensity. For the
exponential kernel the integral is closed-form,
$\int_0^T\lambda=\mu T+\frac{\alpha}{\beta}\sum_i(1-e^{-\beta(T-t_i)})$,
and the log terms use Ogata's recursion: with
$A_i=\sum_{j<i}e^{-\beta(t_i-t_j)}$,
\begin{equation}
A_i=e^{-\beta(t_i-t_{i-1})}\,(1+A_{i-1}),\qquad A_1=0,\qquad
\lambda(t_i)=\mu+\alpha A_i .
\label{eq:recursion}
\end{equation}
\emph{Worked micro-example.} Take $\beta=1$ and events at
$t=(0,\,0.5,\,2)$. Then $A_1=0$;
$A_2=e^{-0.5}(1+0)=0.607$;
$A_3=e^{-1.5}(1+0.607)=0.359$: the recursion carries the total decayed
excitation forward in one multiplication per event, so each likelihood
evaluation costs $O(n)$ instead of the naive $O(n^2)$---the property that
makes MLE feasible on $10^5$--$10^6$ events. Estimation maximises
\eqref{eq:ll} over $(\log\mu,\log\alpha,\log\beta)$---the log
parametrisation makes positivity implicit---with the L-BFGS-B quasi-Newton
method.

\subsection{Goodness of fit: the time-rescaling theorem}
\label{sec:theory-rescaling}
Let $\Lambda(t)=\int_0^t\lambda(s)\,ds$ be the \keyword{compensator}. The
\keyword{time-rescaling theorem}~\cite{timerescaling} states that if
$\{t_i\}$ is generated by intensity $\lambda$, the rescaled times
$\tau_i=\Lambda(t_i)$ form a unit-rate Poisson process; equivalently the
increments $\Delta\tau_i=\Lambda(t_i)-\Lambda(t_{i-1})$ are i.i.d.\
$\mathrm{Exp}(1)$. Model checking thus reduces to a distributional test of
the $\Delta\tau_i$ against $\mathrm{Exp}(1)$: a quantile--quantile plot
(points on the diagonal $=$ good fit; the \emph{location} of departures
diagnoses \emph{where} the model errs) and a Kolmogorov--Smirnov statistic.
Two practical rules applied in this work: the sample mean of the
$\Delta\tau_i$ equals $1$ under any correctly normalised compensator,
providing an independent implementation check; and at $n\sim10^5$ the KS
test rejects every parametric model (its power grows without bound), so
the QQ geometry and the KS \emph{magnitude}---not the $p$-value---carry the
meaning.

\subsection{Simulation: Ogata thinning}
Validating an estimator requires data with known parameters. Ogata's
thinning~\cite{ogata} simulates exactly: from time $t$ with current bound
$M\ge\lambda$, draw a candidate gap $\sim\mathrm{Exp}(M)$, accept the
candidate with probability $\lambda(\text{candidate})/M$, update the
intensity on acceptance, repeat. Estimator verification by
simulate-then-refit is a \emph{gate} in this project: no fit on real data
is reported unless the estimator provably recovers known parameters
(\S\ref{sec:impl-hawkes}).

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Ogata thinning for the exponential-kernel Hawkes
process (as implemented and tested).},label={lst:thinning}]
def simulate(mu, alpha, beta, T, seed=None):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    events, t, excite = [], 0.0, 0.0     # excite = sum of decayed jumps
    while True:
        M = mu + excite                   # intensity upper bound at t+
        t_new = t + rng.exponential(1.0 / M)
        if t_new > T: break
        excite *= np.exp(-beta * (t_new - t))   # decay to candidate
        if rng.uniform() <= (mu + excite) / M:  # accept w.p. lambda/M
            events.append(t_new)
            excite += alpha                     # the new event's jump
        t = t_new
    return np.asarray(events)
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Extreme-value theory for alert thresholds}
\label{sec:theory-evt}
The alerting problem: choose a threshold $z_q$ exceeded by a target
fraction $q$ of scores, and keep it valid as the score distribution
drifts. EVT provides the tail model. By the Pickands--Balkema--de~Haan
theorem~\cite{pickands,coles}, for a broad class of distributions the
exceedances over a high threshold $t$ converge in law to the
\keyword{Generalized Pareto Distribution}
\[
\Pr[X-t>x\mid X>t]\;\approx\;\Bigl(1+\frac{\gamma x}{\sigma}\Bigr)^{-1/\gamma},
\]
with shape $\gamma$ and scale $\sigma$. Writing $n$ for the sample size and
$N_t$ for the number of exceedances, the unconditional tail is
$\Pr[X>z]\approx\frac{N_t}{n}\bigl(1+\gamma(z-t)/\sigma\bigr)^{-1/\gamma}$;
setting it equal to $q$ and solving for $z$ gives the \keyword{POT
threshold}
\begin{equation}
z_q=t+\frac{\sigma}{\gamma}\Bigl[\Bigl(\frac{qn}{N_t}\Bigr)^{-\gamma}-1\Bigr].
\label{eq:zq}
\end{equation}
For streaming use, $(t,\gamma,\sigma)$ are re-estimated on a trailing
window while $q$ stays fixed---the SPOT construction~\cite{spot}. A fast
estimator suited to constant-time updates is the method of moments: with
$m$ and $v$ the mean and variance of the exceedances,
\[
\hat\gamma=\tfrac12\Bigl(1-\frac{m^2}{v}\Bigr),\qquad
\hat\sigma=m\,(1-\hat\gamma).
\]
The theorem's continuity hypothesis is not decorative: on score
distributions with large atoms (heavy ties) the GPD approximation of the
tail degrades and \eqref{eq:zq} can extrapolate absurdly---a failure mode
met, diagnosed, and handled in \S\ref{sec:impl-evt}, where a
\emph{distribution-free} alternative (the empirical $(1{-}q)$-quantile of a
trailing window) is substituted.

\section{Feature construction}
\label{sec:method-features}
\paragraph{HDFS: block sessions.} The unit is the block session (labels are
per block). Each session is the count vector
$x_u\in\mathbb{N}^{48}$ of its template occurrences, transformed by
$\log(1{+}x)$; for the autoencoder, standardisation uses moments of the
\emph{normal training} population only.

\paragraph{BGL: fixed windows, two feature blocks.} Lacking a session key,
the stream is cut into 60-second windows. The \emph{content} block counts
the top-50 templates (vocabulary fixed on the training period). The
\emph{timing} block summarises the Hawkes excitation level
$A(t)=\sum_{t_i<t}e^{-\beta(t-t_i)}$ along the window (mean and maximum) at
two decay scales ($1/\beta=1$\,s and $30$\,s), plus the event count; it is
computed from event \emph{times only}---labels never enter---so it is a
legitimate feature under the leakage rules. A window is labelled anomalous
(for evaluation only) if it contains at least one alert line.

\section{Validation protocol}
\label{sec:validation-protocol}
Fixed before any model was trained:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item \textbf{Temporal split}: units ordered by start time; earliest 70\%
  train, latest 30\% test; never shuffled. On HDFS: boundary at
  2008-11-11~06:00:46; 402{,}543 training vs.\ 172{,}518 test blocks.
  \item \textbf{Unsupervised training}: the autoencoder sees only the
  389{,}428 \emph{normal training} blocks; scalers and vocabularies are
  fitted on training data only.
  \item \textbf{Metrics}: PR-AUC; precision and false alarms/day at recall
  $0.90$; durations measured, not assumed.
  \item \textbf{Drift}: all metrics recomputed on consecutive chronological
  slices of the test period, with thresholds fixed once.
  \item \textbf{Single harness}: every detector emits ``higher = more
  anomalous'' scores evaluated by the same code.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Software engineering and quality assurance}
\label{sec:method-engineering}
Development followed an incremental, test-gated workflow under git.
The automated suite (34 tests) covers the metric implementations
(operating points and FA/day on synthetic cases with known answers), the

\section{Risk management}

\section{Exploratory analysis: three findings that shaped the design}
\label{sec:impl-eda}

\paragraph{Finding 1 --- events cluster massively.} The Fano factor
(interval-count variance/mean; $=1$ for Poisson) measures $5.3$ on HDFS
per-minute counts and $698$--$2286$ on BGL: one to three orders of
magnitude above memorylessness. Self-excitation is a first-class property
of the data, established \emph{before} any model
(Figure~\ref{fig:eda}, left).

\paragraph{Finding 2 --- HDFS cannot host a continuous-time model.} HDFS
stamps time to the second at $\approx80$ events/s. Measured: \textbf{98.8\%}
of consecutive events tie globally; \textbf{90.5\%} tie \emph{within} a
block; every block lives $\le54$\,s (median 7\,s) over a median of
$\approx2$ distinct seconds. A continuous-time process requires an order
that does not exist here. Artificial jitter was rejected---the estimated
branching ratio would measure the jitter, not the system---and the Hawkes
analysis was relocated to BGL (microsecond stamps; $0\%$ ties). This
mid-project redirection, forced by measurement, is presented as a result in
its own right.

\paragraph{Finding 3 --- the base rate drifts.} Under the temporal split
the anomaly rate falls from $3.26\%$ (training period) to $2.16\%$ (test
period): non-stationarity is present even in the labels, anticipating the
drift analyses below. The block-size distribution (median 19 events) has a
long anomaly-rich tail (Figure~\ref{fig:eda}, right).

\section{The Hawkes timing model on BGL}
\label{sec:impl-hawkes}

\subsection{Estimator verification (gate)}
Before real data: trajectories simulated by Ogata thinning with known
$(\mu,\alpha,\beta)$ are refit---parameters recovered within a few
percent; the rescaling diagnostic accepts the true model
(KS $\approx0.01$, mean gap $\approx1$) and rejects a deliberately wrong
one. Listing~\ref{lst:recursion} shows the core recursion exactly as
implemented and tested.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={The $O(n)$ likelihood recursion (project source).},
label={lst:recursion}]
def _recursion_A(t: np.ndarray, beta: float) -> np.ndarray:
    """A_i = e^{-beta (t_i - t_{i-1})} (1 + A_{i-1}), A_0 = 0."""
    A = np.empty_like(t)
    A[0] = 0.0
    for i in range(1, len(t)):
        A[i] = np.exp(-beta * (t[i] - t[i-1])) * (1.0 + A[i-1])
    return A

def neg_log_likelihood(params, t, T):
    mu, alpha, beta = np.exp(params)          # log-parametrisation
    lam = mu + alpha * _recursion_A(t, beta)  # intensities at events
    integral = mu*T + (alpha/beta) * np.sum(1 - np.exp(-beta*(T - t)))
    return -(np.sum(np.log(lam)) - integral)
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Interpretation}
The branching ratio is \emph{near-critical} on both windows
($\eta\approx0.97$--$1.0$): each event triggers on the order of one direct
offspring; by the cluster representation, mean cascade size
$1/(1-\eta)$ is enormous---the stream operates at the edge of
self-sustaining avalanches. This is the project's central quantitative
statement about the data, and it is \emph{validated}: the rescaled-gap
mean of exactly $1.000$ certifies the compensator, and the QQ body on the
diagonal certifies the bulk fit. The diagnostics also \emph{localise} the
model's failure: the upper tail departs upward---the longest quiet gaps
exceed what one exponential timescale allows. After a burst the fitted
kernel expects continued activity; when silence follows, the integrated
intensity over the gap is inflated. Mild when calm (KS 0.095), severe in
the cascade (KS 0.431). Conclusion: the exponential Hawkes captures the
clustering to first order, and its own diagnostic argues for a multi-scale
(sum-of-exponentials) kernel---future work rather than a defect of method.

\section{Adaptive thresholding under drift}
\label{sec:impl-evt}

\subsection{EVT: verification and a documented failure mode}
The POT threshold \eqref{eq:zq} with the method-of-moments GPD estimator
was verified on simulation: target exceedance rates held on stationary
streams; drift tracked where a fixed threshold fails. On the real HDFS
scores, however, extrapolation misbehaved---thresholds could fall below the
bulk. Diagnosis: \textbf{58.7\%} of normal test blocks share one
\emph{identical} score value (normal sessions are so stereotyped that whole
cohorts have byte-identical count vectors); the score law has large atoms,
violating the continuity hypothesis of \S\ref{sec:theory-evt}. The design
response: on HDFS, replace the parametric tail by the
\emph{distribution-free} trailing-window quantile; reserve EVT for
continuous scores (e.g.\ the BGL timing features). Reporting this failure
mode is deliberate: knowing \emph{when a tool's hypotheses fail} is as
valuable as the tool. Listing~\ref{lst:rolling} shows the substituted
mechanism in full---its simplicity is the point: it assumes nothing about
the score distribution.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Distribution-free adaptive threshold (trailing
window quantile), as deployed on HDFS.},label={lst:rolling}]
class RollingQuantile:
    """Alert threshold = empirical (1-q)-quantile of a trailing window,
    recomputed every `stride` observations."""
    def __init__(self, q=0.02, window=20_000, stride=500):
        self.q, self.window, self.stride = q, window, stride
    def fit(self, calib):
        self._buf = list(calib[-self.window:])
        self.z = float(np.quantile(self._buf, 1 - self.q))
        return self
    def run(self, scores):
        flags, thr, since = [], [], 0
        for x in scores:
            flags.append(x > self.z)          # alert decision first
            self._buf.append(float(x))        # then update the window
            del self._buf[:-self.window]
            since += 1
            if since >= self.stride:          # periodic recalibration
                self.z = float(np.quantile(self._buf, 1 - self.q))
                since = 0
            thr.append(self.z)
        return np.array(flags), np.array(thr)
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Triage: from alerts to ranked incidents}
\label{sec:impl-triage}
The agent applies \emph{deterministic reduction, then explanation}:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item \textbf{Deduplication} --- flags with identical signature (entity +
  template multiset) merge with a count;
  \item \textbf{Correlation} --- temporally clustered flags form one
  incident: the operational translation of near-critical
  self-excitation (clustered anomalies are cascade members sharing a root
  cause);
  \item \textbf{Explanation and ranking} --- each incident is emitted as
  strict JSON and ranked P1$\to$P4. The backend is pluggable: a
  deterministic rule engine (always available) or an LLM constrained by the
  same schema; one call per \emph{incident}, the reduction paying for the
  model.
\end{enumerate}
On the 200 highest-scoring HDFS test blocks: \textbf{90 incidents}
(P1:13, P2:53, P3:2, P4:22; categories: data-loss 54,
replication-failure 30, network 6). Listing~\ref{lst:incident} shows one
output object.

\section{Limitations and threats to validity}
\label{sec:limitations}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item \textbf{Reproduction, not novelty, on HDFS detection.} The 0.987
  figure validates the pipeline; the literature contains many equivalent
  results.
  \item \textbf{Absolute difficulty of BGL windows.} PR-AUC $\approx0.09$
  (vs.\ 0.047 chance) is weak in absolute terms; the timing gain is
  relative and is presented as such.
  \item \textbf{Kernel misfit in the tail.} The exponential kernel is
  rejected in the upper tail by its own diagnostic; the cascade-window
  $\eta\approx0.9998$ must be read with that misfit in mind.
  \item \textbf{EVT inapplicable to discrete scores.} On HDFS the GPD
  hypothesis fails (58.7\% identical scores); the distribution-free
  alternative was used. EVT's intended domain here (continuous timing
  scores) was verified on simulation, not yet in production-like streams.
  \item \textbf{LLM backend not exercised live} in the reported runs
  (no credential in the development environment).
  \item \textbf{Hyperparameters fixed a priori} (window 60\,s, top-50
  vocabulary, two decay scales, architecture); no sweep was performed, so
  reported numbers are conservative.
  \item \textbf{Public data as proxy.} Transfer to an internal log source
  ---formats, volumes, drift regimes---is prepared by the design (generic
  structured-log interface) but not performed within this internship.
  \item \textbf{Single-run variance.} Isolation-Forest results carry
  run-to-run randomness; the ablation's PR-AUC gaps ($\pm0.006$) are within
  that noise, which is why the argument rests on the operating-point
  (false-alarm) improvement rather than on third-decimal AUC differences.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
This internship delivered a complete, reproducible log-anomaly pipeline
whose stages answer, term for term, the structural difficulties of
industrial log analysis identified in Part~I: unsupervised detection for
the absence of labels; a validated self-exciting timing model for the
cascade structure of incidents; drift-robust alerting and LLM-assisted
triage for the volume asymmetry. Scientifically, the central results are a
near-critical, rescaling-validated branching ratio on BGL; a measured
halving of false alarms attributable to timing features; and a
demonstrated stabilisation of the false-alarm rate under drift.
Methodologically, the through-line---synthetic verification before real
data, strictly out-of-time evaluation, operationally meaningful headline
metrics, drift measured rather than averaged away, and negative findings
reported as first-class results---is what makes each number in this report
defensible, and is, in the author's view, the internship's most durable
outcome.

\section{Perspectives}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item \textbf{Multi-scale kernels.} Sum-of-exponentials or power-law
  excitation to resolve the upper-tail misfit; model selection by the same
  rescaling diagnostics.
  \item \textbf{Rescaling residuals as a detector.} The fitted model turns
  the stream into calibrated $\mathrm{Exp}(1)$ material; departures are
  continuous anomaly scores---squarely in EVT's domain, closing the loop
  between Chapters~\ref{ch:theory} and~\ref{ch:implementation}.
  \item \textbf{Systematic sweeps.} Window length, vocabulary, decay
  scales, detector architecture, with the ablation harness as the
  evaluator.
  \item \textbf{Transfer to an internal source.} Deploy the pipeline on an
  operational log stream of the host entity, exercise the LLM backend
  live, and measure the end metric that matters: analyst time saved per
  incident.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Glossary}
\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt,style=nextline,itemsep=1pt]
\item[Template / event type] The fixed shape of a family of log lines with
variable tokens masked; produced by Drain.
\item[Block session (HDFS)] All lines referencing one HDFS block; the
labelled unit of analysis.
\item[Count vector] Representation of a unit by its per-template
occurrence counts.
\item[Autoencoder] Bottlenecked network trained to reconstruct its input;
reconstruction error serves as anomaly score.
\item[Isolation Forest] Ensemble detector scoring by ease of isolation
under random splits.
\item[PR-AUC] Area under the precision--recall curve; chance level equals
the positive rate.
\item[False alarms per day] False positives above the threshold achieving a
fixed recall, divided by the measured test duration.
\item[Temporal split] Train on the earliest period, test on the strictly
later one; never shuffled.
\item[Hawkes process] Self-exciting point process; each event raises the
intensity with decaying effect.
\item[Branching ratio] Mean number of direct offspring per event;
$<1$ stable, $\ge1$ explosive.
\item[Compensator / time-rescaling] Integrated intensity; under a correct
model, rescaled gaps are $\mathrm{Exp}(1)$---the basis of the
goodness-of-fit test.
\item[EVT / POT / GPD] Extreme-value machinery for principled tail
thresholds.
\item[Drift] Temporal change of the data or score distribution; measured
per period.
\end{description}
